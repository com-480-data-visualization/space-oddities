\begin{summary}
    This project, \textit{Space Oddities}, explores the historical growth and current density of artificial objects in Earth's orbit. By combining metadata from the Union of Concerned Scientists with orbital elements from Space-Track, we aim to visualize the risk of orbital congestion and the emergence of "mega-constellations" like Starlink. Our visualization will guide users from a historical overview of space launches to a visual mapping of collision risks in Low Earth Orbit (LEO).
\end{summary}

\section{Dataset}

\subsection{Data Sources}
To ensure a comprehensive analysis, we will use three primary datasets:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{UCS Satellite Database} \cite{ucs-database}: A comprehensive registry detailing active satellites. It provides crucial metadata including ownership, country of origin, purpose, mass, and orbit class.
    \item \textbf{Space-Track.org} \cite{space-track}: Maintained by the US Space Command, this platform provides raw spatial data via Two-Line Elements (TLEs) for the entire catalog of tracked orbital objects, including active payloads and debris.
    \item \textbf{CelesTrak} \cite{celestrak}: Maintained by Dr. T. S. Kelso, this dataset serves as an external source to compare and augment results from Space-Track.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Quality \& Preprocessing}
The UCS database is highly structured but was last updated in May 2023, requiring careful synchronization with current orbital data. Space-Track data is updated daily but requires complex parsing of TLE text formats.

The UCS database contains \textbf{7,560 rows} and 28 substantive columns; mass data is approximately 2\% incomplete. CelesTrak's SATCAT catalogs 68,147 total tracked objects across 17 columns. We will use Python libraries such as \texttt{Skyfield} or \texttt{SGP4} to convert raw TLEs into metrics such as altitude, inclination, and semi-major axis. The primary challenge will be joining metadata from the UCS with spatial data using unique NORAD Catalog IDs, and complementing this information with sources like CelesTrak.

\section{Problematic}

\begin{fquote}[Donald J. Kessler][][\cite{kessler-syndrome}]
    The orbit of the Earth is a limited resource. As we place more and more objects into it, the probability of collisions will increase until the collision rate exceeds the rate of removal by atmospheric drag.
\end{fquote}

\subsection{Topic \& Main Axis}
Our visualization explores the exponential growth of orbital objects, focusing on congestion and the threat of the "Kessler Syndrome" \cite{kessler-syndrome}. We aim to answer:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Where} is the highest density of objects currently located?
    \item \textbf{How} have mega-constellations altered the landscape of LEO?
    \item \textbf{What} are the current risks of collision ?
\end{itemize}

\begin{invsummary}
    \textbf{The Kessler Syndrome:} A theoretical scenario where the density of objects in LEO is high enough that collisions between objects cause a cascade of debris, potentially making specific orbits unusable for generations.
\end{invsummary}

\subsection{Motivation \& Target Audience}
With the commercial spaceflight boom, space sustainability has become a critical global issue. Our target audience includes the general public and policymakers who require an intuitive understanding of orbital density that raw data alone cannot provide.

\section{Exploratory Data Analysis}
\subsection{Initial Statistics}
Initial exploration reveals a space environment under growing stress. The UCS database catalogs \textbf{7,560 active satellites}, of which 89.5\% reside in LEO. Launch activity has grown dramatically: 2022 alone saw 2,113 new satellites — more than all launches before 2018 combined (see Figure~\ref{fig:launches}). The driving force is the deployment of mega-constellations such as Starlink, which now account for the majority of active LEO objects.

\section{Related work}
\subsection{Existing Visualization Projects}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stuff in Space} \cite{stuff-in-space}: A 3D WebGL visualization showing real-time positions. While technically impressive, it lacks a guided narrative.
    \item \textbf{LeoLabs} \cite{leolabs}: A commercial platform for conjunction monitoring. However, it is primarily a proprietary tool for satellite operators.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Originality \& Inspiration}
Our approach differs by being \textbf{narrative-driven}. We will not just plot 3D points; we will integrate 2D temporal charts with a filtered 3D view to visualize "orbital highways." By incorporating risk-based data (CDMs), we uniquely visualize the \textit{potential} for disaster rather than just the \textit{presence} of objects. We draw inspiration from the aesthetic of \textit{Stuff in Space} but intend to provide the data depth of commercial tools like LeoLabs in a publicly accessible, educational format. Esri's ArcGIS satellite map \cite{esri-satellite} also demonstrates the power of a curated 3D orbit view, which informs our design.

\subsection{Visual Inspiration}
Beyond space-specific tools, we look to broader data journalism for narrative and aesthetic guidance:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Pudding} (\texttt{pudding.cool}) — a primary structural inspiration for scrollytelling that guides users through a data-driven story step by step.
    \item \textbf{Information is Beautiful} (\texttt{informationisbeautiful.net}) — inspires the layered, dense visual language we aim to achieve for showing relative object populations.
    \item \textbf{ESA Space Debris Office} — their debris evolution charts and belt cross-sections provide a benchmark for representing orbital density as a continuous quantity rather than discrete points.
\end{itemize}
